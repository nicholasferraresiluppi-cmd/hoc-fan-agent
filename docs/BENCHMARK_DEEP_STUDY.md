# Benchmark deep study — HOC Pro vs best-in-class (luglio 2026)

> Studio di prodotto commissionato da Nicholas (2026-07-18) a valle della ricerca di mercato del 2026-07-17.
> Metodo: 12 studi paralleli per-prodotto (fonti primarie dove aperte, triangolazione indiretta dove demo-gated), 10 verifiche avversariali sui claim più decisionali, critica di completezza, più sessione hands-on nel browser (demo pubbliche e tour). Nessun signup, nessun contatto commerciale, nessun dato HOC condiviso con terzi.
> Ogni pattern porta: verdetto (STEAL / ADAPT / IGNORE), effort (S/M/L su stack Next.js+KV), impact 1-5, confidence, e la marcatura **osservato** (docs/demo primarie) vs **inferito** (ricostruito da fonti secondarie). I dossier per-prodotto sono in inglese (output diretto degli agenti di ricerca); sintesi e raccomandazioni in italiano.
> Prezzi: sempre "as of July 2026", quasi tutti da fonti terze (i vendor enterprise non pubblicano listini).

## 0. Executive summary

**Nessun prodotto sul mercato fa quello che fa HOC Pro come bundle** (conferma dello studio di mercato): lo strato OFM-native si ferma a KPI tables e shift scheduling; nessuno ha score composito versionato, action center HR, comp tiers, career ladder o simulatore di training. Lo studio quindi non produce "un competitor da copiare" ma **~90 pattern di design da 12 prodotti best-in-class per singolo modulo**, di cui 30+ con verdetto STEAL.

Le cinque convergenze più forti (indipendenti tra loro, trovate in 3+ prodotti ciascuna):

1. **Lo score di qualità conversazionale è un oggetto standard del settore** (Zendesk QA/IQS, MaestroQA, Level AI, Centrical QM) e a HOC Pro manca del tutto: score v3 è outcome-heavy (85/15 con proxy qualità). La forma canonica: scorecard versionata a categorie pesate + categoria "critical" che azzera (compliance) + pass-rate come secondo asse + root-cause tags + evidenze allegate a ogni voto.
2. **Rubrica condivisa tra training e live** (Hyperbound "one scorecard, two arenas", Zenarate Call Analyzer, Centrical AI Role-Play): la stessa rubrica valuta simulazione e lavoro reale, così il punteggio di training predice qualcosa. Oggi Academy e leaderboard HOC non condividono vocabolario.
3. **Coaching come oggetto, mai note libere**: sessione con pre-work del coachee (Ambition), evidenze allegate, action plan tracciati, **acknowledgement + diritto di replica** dell'operatore (Observe.AI, MaestroQA dual sign-off) — la paper trail HR-defensible che manca ad Action/Coaching Center.
4. **Trasparenza comp come albero di derivazione** (CaptivateIQ traceability, Everstage lineage): ogni riga di payout risale a transazione → regola → importo, con inquiry/dispute come oggetto agganciato allo statement, mai come DM.
5. **Il leaderboard globale permanente è un failure mode documentato** (SalesScreen doctrine, Hyperbound, Centrical smart segmentation): i fix convergenti sono target personali (% del proprio obiettivo), competizioni time-boxed, segmentazione per tenure, elementi di chance.

**Il rischio principale emerso** (critica di completezza): quattro gap decisionali stanno A MONTE di qualunque build — (1) lo score Infloww su cui la ladder v0.4 gate-a le promozioni non è mai stato studiato come artefatto (formula, versioning, drift); (2) l'accesso programmatico ai transcript delle chat (prerequisito della gamba qualità) non è mai stato verificato contro l'API ufficiale Infloww; (3) la difendibilità legale dello scoring algoritmico dei worker (EU AI Act — obblighi in fase-in da ago 2026, GDPR art. 22, Statuto dei Lavoratori art. 4) non è coperta da nessuna lente; (4) manca una policy su dispute/retroattività (una correzione riscrive lo storico o vale solo forward?).

## 2. Findings hands-on (browser, prima persona)

- **Hyperbound (demo pubblica)**: authoring = industria × 8 tipi call (incluso "Manager Coaching Call") × persona × **stato emotivo del buyer** (Nice/Less Rude/Rude/Sassy) × 26 lingue. Lo stato emotivo come dimensione ortogonale di configurazione è direttamente trasferibile ai fan AI del simulatore. Call e analytics demo-gated (form + mic).
- **Second Nature (deep link anonimi funzionanti)**: **pass score dichiarato upfront** (80, visibile prima di iniziare), sessione a timer 3', "noisy mode" (rumore ambientale simulato), wrapper a corso con step e checkmark. Voice-only: report post-sessione non osservabile senza mic.
- **CaptivateIQ**: home admin **task-centrica** ("Current tasks: open payout inquiries / pending payout reviews / pending inquiry approvals" con contatori); "Inquiries" è voce di Inbox con badge — la disputa comp trattata come posta in arrivo, non come eccezione.
- **Meta-finding**: tutti i "tour interattivi" del settore (CaptivateIQ/Navattic, Lattice, Second Nature tryme, Hyperbound) sono lead-gated con form nome+email aziendale. Le superfici davvero aperte: help center (Zendesk QA, MaestroQA, Ambition), blog di dottrina (SalesScreen), librerie open (progression.fyi, Medium Snowflake open-source, Monzo framework MIT).
- **Progression**: il workspace pubblico (team.progressionapp.com) non carica — segnale debole coerente coi dubbi sulla salute dell'azienda. Il modello dati resta il riferimento; il tool no.

## 3. Esito delle verifiche avversariali

10 claim portanti sottoposti a refutazione con fonti indipendenti: **1 CONFIRMED pieno** (Centrical QM forms con auto-fail — shipped, referenziato da terzi), **9 PARTIAL** (nucleo del claim corroborato da utenti reali/fonti terze, ma framing marketing da scontare e caveat materiali — dettaglio in Appendice B). Nessun REFUTED: nessun pattern del backlog poggia su un claim smentito. Cautela ricorrente: per i prodotti demo-gated (Centrical, Quantified, Ambition post-relaunch) i meccanismi operativi restano inferiti da materiale vendor; le UI non sono mai state osservate direttamente (gap critica #8 — sanabile solo con un secondo pass con signup autorizzati).

## 4. Gap aperti (critica di completezza — da chiudere prima delle decisioni)

1. **Score Infloww mai studiato** — è il numero su cui CAREER_LADDER v0.4 gate-a le promozioni; formula e versioning sono fuori dal nostro controllo. → mini-dossier + snapshot pesi a ogni import.
2. **Accesso transcript non verificato** — ogni pattern QA presuppone transcript; Infloww è blindata, API ufficiale unica via. → test endpoint + ToS prima di approvare il processo QA della ladder (§8.1).
3. **Difendibilità legale** — AI Act (worker management = high-risk, obblighi da ago 2026), GDPR art. 22, art. 4 Statuto. → memo legale, poi requisiti di prodotto (human-in-the-loop documentato, spiegabilità, appello).
4. **Dispute/retroattività** — decidere: correzione riscrive il mese con audit-log + ri-valutazione gate (raccomandato) vs forward-only. Scriverlo nella ladder doc.
5. **Conflitto TL coach-promoter** — il bonus sviluppo paga il TL per promozione mentre il TL propone le promozioni; nessun prodotto studiato risolve; leggere i 2 patent pubblici su coaching effectiveness (NICE, AmplifAI) e aggiungere regola: il proponente non può essere l'unica fonte di evidenza.
6. **Multi-consumer score architecture non validata da nessuno** — "one scoring model, one audit trail" (Quantified) è marketing inferito; costruire il gaming-signal monitor comunque.
7. **Gate hard vs decision-support** — per band-priority: raccomandato decision-support-with-audit-trail (reversibile, più economico, coerente con la cultura attuale).
8. **Nessuna UI osservata** — autorizzare eventualmente un secondo pass con signup (Hyperbound Free, SalesScreen trial 30gg, Zendesk QA trial).
9. **Ladder evidence sbilanciata su engineering** — approfondire Teleperformance JUMP, COPC, ladder di support org (Automattic, Intercom).
10. **Interazione gamification×comp×HR** — la separation policy di cui sopra.

# Appendice A — Dossier per prodotto

I 12 dossier completi, in inglese, come prodotti dagli agenti di studio (con verdetti, fonti e sezioni "non verificato" per ciascuno).

### Zendesk QA (ex-Klaus) — Tier T1

Zendesk QA is the former Klaus (Estonian conversation-review startup, acquired by Zendesk in early 2024), now sold as an add-on to Zendesk Support: Quality Assurance at $35/agent/month or bundled with Workforce Management in the Workforce Engagement bundle at $50/agent/month (as of July 2026, per third-party pricing roundups; not verified on a first-party pricing page). Users are support/CX teams — QA managers, team leads, peer reviewers, and agents — reviewing helpdesk conversations (text and voice). Core mechanics: conversations sync from the helpdesk every 4-6 hours into workspaces; each workspace has scorecards made of weighted rating categories (integer weights 0-100, scales binary/3/4/5-point); reviewers grade sampled conversations, producing a review score (weighted average of category scores) that rolls up into IQS (mean of review scores) and a pass rate against a configurable baseline (default 80%). Critical categories force a 0% score when rated below 50%. AutoQA autoscore 100% of conversations on 8 built-in AI categories plus up to 10 custom prompt-based LLM categories; Spotlight flags churn risk, escalations, outliers, dead air etc. to route risky conversations into review assignments. Around the score sit a coaching layer (pins, coaching sessions, action items), a formal dispute flow with partial acceptance, and calibration sessions for inter-rater reliability. Klaus's benchmark survey (4,000+ support professionals, 2023) set the industry IQS reference at 88%.

**Pattern rilevati** (verdetto · effort · impact 1-5 · confidence · osservato/inferito):

#### Weighted scorecard data model with exact IQS math and prospective-only versioning
`STEAL` · effort M · impact 5/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Workspaces segment teams, each with its own scorecards and QA threshold. A scorecard = ordered rating categories (optionally grouped in sections); each category has a rating scale (binary maps to 0/100; 3-point 0/50/100; 4-point 0/33.3/66.6/100; 5-point 0/25/50/75/100), an integer weight 0-100 (default 10; hovering shows the weight's % contribution to the total score), and optional critical flag. Formulas: category_score = (selected - scale_min)/(scale_max - scale_min)*100; review_score = sum(cat_score*weight)/sum(weights) over rated categories; IQS = mean of review scores as %. N/A categories are excluded without re-normalizing the remaining weights; if all N/A, no score. Conditional scorecards auto-apply by source type, channel, helpdesk tag, or CSAT score. Category edits apply to future conversations only; categories can be deactivated (history kept in reporting) vs deleted (history erased) — versioning discipline baked into the data model.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** This is exactly the shape of the conversation-quality leg HOC's score v3 lacks (today quality is a 15% proxy). The KV translation is direct: a versioned scorecard config object (mirroring how leaderboard-config.js already versions score changes), per-conversation review records, and IQS-style rollup per chatter per period that could blend into SCORE_WEIGHTS. The N/A-exclusion rule and prospective-only edits solve two problems HOC already codified elsewhere (MIN_SHIFTS_RELIABLE, 'never change score without versioning').

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043701093786-About-the-Zendesk-QA-Reviews-dashboard-calculations> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043760215194-Creating-scorecards> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/8992409602842-Viewing-and-managing-rating-categories-for-scorecards> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/10093676975898-Getting-started-with-Zendesk-QA-Admin-guide>

#### Critical fail-all categories plus pass rate as a second axis distinct from the average score
`STEAL` · effort S · impact 4/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Any category can be flagged critical: a rating below 50% on it forces the whole review score to 0% (or, if the category sits in a group, admins choose fail-entire-review vs fail-group-only). Separately, a global account setting defines a pass baseline (default 80%): reviews scoring >= baseline pass, and pass rate = passed reviews / total reviews. Changing the baseline recolors historical and new data. Dashboards show pass rate globally, per reviewer, per reviewee, and per review (color-coded), so a team can have a decent average IQS but a visible tail of failed interactions.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** HOC's leaderboard averages hide catastrophic single conversations, which in OFM are existential (PII leaks, promising real-life meetings, chargeback-baiting, underage red flags). A critical-category floor plus a pass-rate metric next to the score gives the Action Center a compliance axis the blended average can never express. Small build: one flag on the category model, one baseline constant, one derived metric.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043668650266-Monitoring-global-and-individual-pass-rates-in-Zendesk-QA> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043760215194-Creating-scorecards>

#### AutoQA built-in categories plus prompt-based custom AI categories with a validation harness
`ADAPT` · effort L · impact 5/5 · conf. 90% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

AutoQA autoscores 100% of conversations on 8 built-in text categories — Greeting, Closing, Spelling & Grammar, Readability (rule-based); Empathy, Tone, Comprehension, Solution Offered (LLM, opt-in to OpenAI processing) — with binary or fixed scales and per-agent vs per-conversation aggregation rules (e.g. conversation gets Greeting upvote only if all agents greeted; Empathy positive if >= 1 agent shows it with no negatives). Greeting/Closing/Spelling are customizable with approved phrases and exemptions. Beyond built-ins: exact-text-match categories and up to 10 active prompt-based AI categories (natural-language prompt <= 2,000 chars, choice of 4 scales). The key mechanism is the validation loop before activation: 'Enhance' rewrites ambiguous prompts, 'Validate' critiques them, then 'Test prompt' runs against up to 100 closed conversations from the last 7 days which the admin manually scores, producing a match rate; prompt version history shows match rates side by side before finalizing.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** HOC cannot use Zendesk's built-ins (Empathy/Greeting are wrong axes for sexting-driven sales chat) and must define its own categories (PPV pitch timing, persona consistency, upsell escalation, compliance red flags) via the Anthropic SDK it already ships. What to copy is the harness, not the categories: prompt-per-category with versioned prompts, a labeled test set of historical chats, and a match-rate gate before a category is allowed to touch anyone's score. That is the difference between a defensible AI quality leg and a vibes-based one, and it maps 1:1 onto the existing simulator scoring code.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043747123354-Understanding-autoscoring-categories> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/9277382490138-Creating-AI-prompt-based-rating-categories> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/9070424158106-Customizing-AutoQA-system-categories>

#### Root-cause tags attached to ratings
`STEAL` · effort S · impact 4/5 · conf. 88% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Each scorecard category can require or offer root causes when rated: admin-defined tag lists, optionally separate lists for positive vs negative ratings, multi-select allowed, plus an 'Other' option with free-text comment. Root causes aggregate in category dashboards, so a low Tone score decomposes into named, countable reasons instead of an opaque number, and recurring causes become coaching topics.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** This is the 'evidence attachment' piece of a quality leg: when a score drives HR action (Action Center export) HOC needs the why stored next to the number. A KV-backed tag taxonomy per category, enforced on negative ratings, gives Coaching Center a data-driven topic list and gives disputed scores an audit trail. Trivial data model, disproportionate defensibility payoff.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043760215194-Creating-scorecards> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/10093676975898-Getting-started-with-Zendesk-QA-Admin-guide>

#### Coaching session as a first-class object: pins to sessions to action items to score trend
`STEAL` · effort M · impact 5/5 · conf. 90% · *observed* · lente: `coaching-object`

Reviewers/leads pin conversations or dashboard findings as private notes whenever they spot a trend ('pin now, discuss at next 1:1'). A coaching session object has coach (creator by default, delegable), coachee, name, date; content is assembled from pinned items pulled in from a side panel, talking points, shared notes plus personal notes visible only to the coach; sessions support templates. During/after the session the coach sets action items for accountability; a Sessions overview page tracks completed, upcoming, and overdue sessions per workspace and per user, and the session's performance panel displays the coachee's IQS so the coach can eyeball score trend around coaching (display-level attribution, not causal). The system also tracks whether agents have viewed their received feedback.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** HOC's Coaching Center can adopt this loop wholesale: pin bad conversations/KPI anomalies from leaderboard drill-downs, bundle into a session object in KV (coach, chatter, pins, action items, date), overdue tracking, and the chatter's score sparkline rendered inside the session view. It converts coaching from an ad-hoc chat into an auditable object that the career ladder and Action Center can reference ('coached twice on X before HR escalation'). Note Zendesk's honest limitation: attribution is a displayed trend, not causal math — HOC should copy that humility.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043747156506-Understanding-coaching-sessions> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043759543194-Using-pinned-notes-to-coach-agents> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/10093676975898-Getting-started-with-Zendesk-QA-Admin-guide>

#### Formal dispute flow with proposed re-scores, partial acceptance, and a disputes dashboard
`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 4/5 · conf. 85% · *observed* · lente: `comp-transparency`

Agents can dispute reviews of their own work; admins/workspace managers/leads can dispute any review. Two dispute types: (1) review content — with optional proposed new scores per category and a mandatory explanatory comment; (2) wrong reviewee — suggesting who should have been reviewed. The disputer chooses the recipient: the original reviewer or an escalation to another team member. The recipient accepts, partially accepts (changing only some of the suggested scores), or rejects, with comments; both parties get notifications. A disputes dashboard tracks dispute volume, average resolution time, most-disputed categories, and reviewer-reviewee alignment over time.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** In HOC a score is money and career: once the quality leg touches comp and ladder gates, chatters need a formal appeal channel or every low score becomes a Telegram argument. Adapt rather than steal: HOC's disputes would target score/period records (and eventually comp lines), not just reviews, and should route to the CM or sales manager with the partial-accept option preserved (it is what makes reviewers actually engage instead of binary-rejecting). The disputes dashboard doubles as a QA-of-QA metric: which categories generate disagreement tells you which rubric lines are ambiguous.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043760048922-Disputing-a-review> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/9642557697306-Responding-to-disputes-in-Zendesk-QA> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7849332023322-How-do-I-partially-accept-a-dispute-in-Zendesk-QA> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/9642514690458-Accessing-and-viewing-the-disputes-dashboard>

#### Calibration sessions: blind co-grading with a baseline review, excluded from live scores
`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 3/5 · conf. 88% · *observed* · lente: `ladder-gates`

A workspace toggle enables calibration; sessions are created with a name and due date, conversations are added, and multiple reviewers grade the same conversations. Visibility controls enforce blindness: previously-given reviews can be hidden from the session, and reviewers can be blocked from seeing peers' calibration reviews until they submit their own (with separate visibility rules for leads/managers); a related workspace-level 'unbiased grading' toggle hides others' scores during normal reviewing too. One review can be marked as the baseline for comparison. Calibration results live in a dedicated dashboard and explicitly do not affect IQS or trigger notifications.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** The career ladder gates promotions on scores; the moment a human-reviewed quality leg joins that gate, HOC must be able to show reviewers agree with each other, or the first contested promotion/demotion collapses the system's credibility. A lightweight version — same 5 conversations graded blind by CMs/QA monthly, variance vs a designated baseline stored in KV — is enough evidence of inter-rater reliability for HR defensibility. Adapt, not steal: at 5-10 reviewers HOC needs a recurring ritual plus a variance readout, not the full session machinery.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043724530842-Setting-up-calibration-in-Zendesk-QA> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/10093676975898-Getting-started-with-Zendesk-QA-Admin-guide>

#### Cycle-based review assignments with coverage goals plus Spotlight risk-first sampling
`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 4/5 · conf. 88% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Assignments run on cycles (daily/weekly/bi-weekly/monthly, custom start, timezone); at cycle start the system fetches all conversations matching conditions (same filter types as conversation search: date, conversation attributes, helpdesk tags; recommended condition 'Review status is not reviewed') and does not refresh until next cycle. Goal types: a pool target (exact number or % of matching conversations), per-reviewer quotas, or per-reviewee minimum coverage (number or % of each agent's conversations). Reviewer selection supports most-active-agent/participant/assignee logic with exclusions; toggles govern conversation replacement and whether reviewers can be assigned their own conversations. Orthogonally, Spotlight auto-flags conversations — churn risk, escalation request, promised follow-up, extra mile, sentiment, outliers (rule-based, e.g. reply count above team average), SLA breach, bot repetition/efficiency, dead air (voice, default 30s threshold), missing recording disclosure — usable as filters/conditions so the riskiest slice of the 98% never manually reviewed gets routed into queues.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** A quality leg is only as fair as its sample: per-chatter minimum coverage per period is the review-side analog of MIN_SHIFTS_RELIABLE and prevents a score built on 2 cherry-picked chats. HOC should adapt the goal model (per-reviewee % coverage + not-reviewed condition + cycle snapshot) and replace Spotlight's insights with OFM-specific flags (refund threats, meet-up requests, spending spikes, ghosting whales) computed by its own LLM pass, feeding the review queue risk-first. Full assignment machinery is overkill at current reviewer headcount; the coverage-goal + risk-filter core is the valuable part.

Fonti: <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043747327770-Setting-up-assignments-in-Zendesk-QA> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/7043759586074-Understanding-spotlight-insights> · <https://support.zendesk.com/hc/en-us/articles/10093676975898-Getting-started-with-Zendesk-QA-Admin-guide>

#### Binary criteria in weighted sections, with two grader types (AI semantic vs keyword match)
`STEAL` · effort S · impact 5/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Scorecard structure: Sections (e.g. 'Opener', 'Closing') contain Criteria, each a strict yes/no question ('Scorecard questions need to be structured as yes/no questions to make sure feedback is actionable'). ### MaestroQA (maestroqa.com; rebranded to Rippit in Feb 2026, old site still fully live) — Tier T1

MaestroQA is a contact-center quality-management platform used by CX/support orgs — QA analysts, team leads, and agents — to grade customer conversations pulled from helpdesks (Zendesk, Salesforce, Intercom) against custom rubrics. #### Blind team calibration with staged per-criterion reveal, quarantined from official scores
`STEAL` · effort M · impact 4/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Admin creates a 'Team Calibration' assignment from selected tickets plus one rubric. Each participating grader scores independently; individual scores stay hidden during this phase to prevent anchoring. A designated final calibrator clicks 'See Scores' and sees only WHO submitted plus their own initial score; then the Final Calibration view shows, for each criterion, every grader's selected option side-by-side, with each grader's overall score at the bottom, and the calibrator sets a consensus score. **Perché per HOC Pro:** Before HOC adds a conversation-quality leg to score v3, the 3-5 people who would grade chats (board now, qa_reviewer role later) must prove they score the same transcript the same way — otherwise the quality leg encodes one person's taste and is indefensible in the Action Center. #### Automation-worthiness triage for scorecard criteria
`ADAPT` · effort S · impact 4/5 · conf. 85% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

A three-filter framework applied per criterion before automating it: (1) business-outcome test — automate only where improving the criterion moves a business metric (their worked example: pushing grammar from 90% to 95% doesn't move CSAT or cost, so don't bother); (2) grading-time-per-question analysis — tabulate minutes spent per criterion (sample given: empathy 2 min, grammar 2 min, knowledge 10 min, correct resolution 10 min) to find where automation buys back the most human time; (3) variance and back-end-dependency test — automate low-variance criteria whose correct answer is consistent across interactions; keep high-variance judgment criteria (e.g. answer correctness depending on backend data) with humans.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** This is the design method for HOC's future LLM quality leg, translated to OFM: automate low-variance detectors (response-time to new fan, PPV follow-up sent, price-anchoring sequence, forbidden-content phrases) and keep high-variance judgment (rapport quality, persona consistency, whale-handling) human. **Perché per HOC Pro:** HOC's Coaching Center can adopt this model nearly verbatim in KV (coaching_session:{id}, coaching_point:{id}, todo:{id} + per-operator indexes). agentConfirmed/agentConfirmedDate is precisely the HR-defensibility trail the Action Center export lacks — proof the operator saw and acknowledged the feedback before any escalation. #### Grader QA with per-criterion Alignment Score
`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 3/5 · conf. 85% · *observed* · lente: `ladder-gates`

#### Rubric structure (standard/bonus/auto-fail sections) with rubric-pre-selected assignment routing
`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 3/5 · conf. 75% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Routing-by-type adapts to chat categories (mass-message follow-up vs sexting session vs renewal save) and to training-sim scenario types. ### SalesScreen (salesscreen.com) — Tier T1

SalesScreen is a Norwegian-founded sales-gamification platform that sits on top of CRM/dialer data (or manual activity reports) and converts sales activity into competitions, celebrations, achievements, coins and a reward shop, broadcast across web, mobile and office TVs ("SalesSurround"). Buyers are B2B sales orgs, banks/insurance, real estate, staffing and call centers — revenue-generating agent populations structurally similar to HOC's chatters. Admins define metrics and launch competitions in ~9 first-class formats (Standard, Target/% of own quota, Custom Target, Battle 1v1, Bracket knockout, Multi-Round with lifelines, King of the Hill, Lottery, Gift Swap, plus Prize Ladder), configure automatic achievements (6 trigger-condition types, XP, streaks with working-day-aware resets) and per-activity coin rules; employees redeem coins in a company-fulfilled Reward Shop, run self-set Missions that colleagues can "boost" with pledged coins, and get instant celebration popups with self-chosen YouTube clips on wins. Pricing as of July 2026: four tiers — Essentials (30-day free trial, 4 metrics, 10 achievements), Scale (min 10 users, adds XP/Missions/Reward Shop), Pro (min 15 users, all competition types + Battles, "most popular"), Enterprise (custom, private DB/SLA); per-user prices are quote-only, third-party estimates ~$30-50/user/month; a Coaching & Scorecards add-on is publicly listed at $5/user/month. Rated 4.5/5 on Capterra (31 reviews); recurring complaints are bugs/wrong numbers, CRM-integration friction, dated mobile app and repetitive celebration content. Their blog doubles as design doctrine: a published five-item failure-mode list (points-as-goal, surveillance feel, burnout, winner-takes-all, hollow recognition) with prescribed fixes and effort-weighted point calibration with weekly rebalancing during rollout.

**Pattern rilevati** (verdetto · effort · impact 1-5 · confidence · osservato/inferito):

#### Target competition: winner = highest % of OWN quota (plus team-relay variant)
`STEAL` · effort S · impact 4/5 · conf. 90% · *observed* · lente: `gamification`

Competition format where each participant races toward their own individually-assigned target; progress bars show % attainment and "the person who reaches the highest percent of their target wins". Targets can be pulled from existing quota objects ("Predefined") or set per-competition ("Custom"); runs for individuals or teams. Vendor positions it explicitly as the fairness format: "Ensure fairness across different roles or team sizes" / level the field between new and experienced reps. Their playbook extends it to a team relay: groups of 3-4 mixed-level reps where "the team earns its reward only when every member hits their individual target", incentivizing strong reps to coach weak ones.

**Perché per HOC Pro:** This is the cleanest answer to HOC's core fairness problem: chatter venduto is dominated by creator assignment, and the score v3 blend only partially compensates. A monthly target competition where each chatter races % of a personal target (derived from their creator mix and history, data already in ops_kpi and creator-aggregates) makes middle performers genuinely competitive without touching the HR-facing score. The relay variant maps directly onto team_lead squads and creates peer-coaching pressure for free. Small build: per-operator target per period in KV + a % attainment leaderboard view.

#### Published failure-mode doctrine with paired fixes (the anti-checklist)
`STEAL` · effort S · impact 5/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `gamification`

SalesScreen publishes an explicit five-item failure-mode list with a prescribed fix per mode: (1) points become the goal — people "split tasks into tiny pieces or chase busywork" → "reward impact rather than raw activity"; (2) surveillance feel — "the moment a system feels like monitoring, trust drops" → "state plainly that the point is visibility and recognition, not control"; (3) burnout — "relentless competition and constant pressure to climb" → "build in rest and celebrate consistency instead of only rewarding peaks"; (4) winner-takes-all — "a single leaderboard that only the top few can win pushes everyone else out" → "personal bests and group goals that keep winning within reach"; (5) hollow recognition — automated-feeling recognition "turns the system into theater" → "tie every reward to something people genuinely value". Rollout method: pilot with one volunteer team, co-design with end users, name surveillance fears directly, "train managers as coaches, not scorekeepers", iterate weekly, scale only after proof of outcome impact (not engagement). **Perché per HOC Pro:** This is the highest-leverage item in the dossier and it costs almost nothing: HOC Pro currently combines a full-population ranked leaderboard with HR consequences (Action Center feeds firing decisions) — the exact surveillance + winner-takes-all combination SalesScreen documents as the way gamification dies. Before onboarding chatters (18-24 month horizon), structurally separate the HR instrument (Action Center, SCORES_VIEW-gated, no game framing) from the motivational layer (small-group, personal-best, chance-based, opt-in). #### Effort-weighted point economy with weekly rebalancing during rollout
`ADAPT` · effort S · impact 4/5 · conf. 90% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`

Point-calibration doctrine: weight points by effort and impact, not ease — "Five quick emails might earn 5 points. An hour of focused writing might earn 50. A difficult conversation you have been avoiding might earn 100, because the resistance was real"; "if checking email earns the same as closing a deal, people optimize for whatever is easiest, and the system quietly trains the wrong habits". Operating cadence: review the system every week in early stages, "rebalance the points that are being gamed, and cut the mechanics that fall flat"; protect streaks with a safety net so one missed day doesn't erase weeks; scale only after demonstrating impact on a business outcome, not engagement with the system itself. **Perché per HOC Pro:** Directly informs what a conversation-quality leg of HOC's score should look like: weight hard high-value behaviors (reviving dead fans, retention saves, upsell sequences) over volume metrics chatters can trivially game (message count — they would split messages exactly as SalesScreen predicts), and always attach a minimum-volume threshold to any percentage-based metric (HOC already has MIN_SHIFTS_RELIABLE=3 as the same idea). ### ladder-tools: Progression (progression.co), Lattice Grow, Deel Engage Career, progression.fyi framework library — Tier T1

Three tools that productize career ladders. Progression (progression.co) is a dedicated career-framework SaaS: reusable Skills defined as per-level requirements, Positions composed as sets of skills-at-required-levels inside Tracks/Teams, an "eagle view" grid juxtaposing all skills x positions x required levels, Check-ins where employee and manager independently rate each required skill (Working towards / Meeting / Exceeding) then reconcile, and Wins — skill-tagged achievement logs that become promotion evidence. Frameworks can be published as public webpages ({company}.progressionapp.com — Monzo's is live and MIT-licensed); progression.fyi is their library of ~75 public frameworks. Pricing not public (two tiers, Platform and Grow; "talk to us") as of July 2026. Lattice Grow is an add-on (~$4/person/month per third parties, as of July 2026) to Lattice's performance suite: Tracks contain Competencies (grouped in themes) with free-text Expectations per level; employees see their assigned track/level, run "Align on expectations" conversations labeling competencies Strength/Opportunity, and open Growth Areas — development objects with actions, due dates, reminders, linkable to review responses. Deel Engage (~$20/employee/month per aggregators, as of July 2026) separates a company-wide Leveling structure from per-team Career frameworks/paths, links Competencies with per-level expectations to paths, offers AI framework generation, and template-based Development plans. Users are HR/People teams at 50-5000-person companies. Recurring real-user complaints: Lattice's dev module feels bolted-on and add-ons stack cost; Deel's career module is shallow (no automated level assignment, managers can't see reports' plans); Progression frameworks are labor-intensive to author.

`ADAPT` · effort M · impact 4/5 · conf. 92% · *observed* · lente: `score-design`
